Campaign to Protect Rural England - Gloucestershire

Bledisloe Cup - 2004 Report by Prof. Adrian Phillips CBE

Large Village: SOUTH CERNEY

I last saw South Cerney in 2001, when it won the Bledisloe Cup in the large village class. It is as good as ever, if not better.

The village is large (3,500 people) and has a wide range of housing development, not all of it is 'picture postcard Cotswolds' at all. Parts of it feel more urban than village-like, but around the older part it retains a strong village feeling with much historic character. What all parts of South Cerney have however is a feeling of being cared for. It matters so much that the whole village - not just the conventionally pretty bits - is well looked after. This must reflect a strong community spirit.

I liked many things about the village, many of which were well documented in the report and identified on the map:

  • The playing fields at Upper Up, which last time sported a fair collection of litter, were impeccable. The new disabled access is welcome and so too is the winding path that leads to the pond: with its wildflowers, darting dragonflies and flitting house martins, this area is a great addition to the village;
  • The war memorial is now in good heart after the repair of past damage to the railings, and the pavements around the nearby shops and school - usually a focus for litter - were spotlessly clean;
  • The new housing at the Limes looks out upon a very well tended green, and there is more of this to be seen at the Piece, with its wild flowers growing happily in the ditches;
  • The new children's playing area at Robert Franklin Way was being well used by mums and children - it would be the envy of many other communities;
  • The churchyard and the thriving allotments are a tribute to the care lavished upon these areas - South Cerney must keep its allotments. Some good new gates have been erected on the footpath leading east from the church, and I hope the kissing gate to the churchyard will soon be reinstated as well;
  • Bow Wow a wonderfully cared for walk - green, cool and watery; it is completely un-municipal in feeling, yet very popular. All the other footpaths 1 saw were also in good condition;
  • The village hall is a design winner, and is now improved through the provision of disabled access to the rear;
  • The notice boards are good — especially the use of a good village map, an idea that more villages should adopt;
  • All the shops, restaurants, pubs and other economic ventures - even a small factory belonging to the Mitie Group - respect their setting;
  • And finally there are dozens of delightful gardens that spill out on to the lanes, footpaths and river Churn.

I found nothing that disappointed, except that I did not see the litter-picking dog I had read about! She (and her owner) have done a great job - and South Cerney has done itself proud.

General remarks

As always, judging the Bledisloe Cup was fascinating and difficult. Maybe this year it was more challenging than usual because of the very different kinds of villages that made it to the final: three from the Cotswolds, one from the Vale of Gloucester and two from the Forest of Dean area.

Villages have been judged against the agreed criteria: these can be summed up in the answer to the question: 'what community has done the most to bring added value to the village environment?' and not - of course - 'what village looks prettiest?' I have tried to take into account that some villages have a much tougher challenge than others, but at the same time, one can only judge by results.

As it turns out, all three winners this year are Cotswold villages, but all the others were impressive runners up. It is really important that they keep trying — who knows, with a little more effort next year all three Bledisloe winners may come from the Vale and Forest of Dean parts of Gloucestershire.

Final Results

I recommend that the following villages should be the winners of the Bledisloe Cup for 2004:

  • Large Village: South Cerney
  • Medium-sized Village: Somerford Keynes
  • Small Village: Cold Aston
